The evolution of graffiti

The oldest known example of modern graffiti is found on traincars created by hobos and railworkers started more than 80 years ago.

Beginning with the tags of the 1960s, a novel overlap of celebrity and anonymity hit the sreets of New York City and Philadelphia. Taggers used coded labels to trace their movements around cities while often alluding to their origins.
And the very illegality of graffity-making that forced it into the shadows also added to its intrigue and growing base of followers.

Within hip hop culture, graffiti have evolved alongside hip hop music, b-boying, and other elements.
Unrelated to hip-hop graffiti, gangs use their own form of graffit to mark territory or to serve as an indicator of gang-related activities.

Graffiti also became associated with the anti-establishment punk rock movement beginning in the 1970s.
Bands widely stenciled their names and logos, while many punk night clubs, squats and hangouts are famous for their graffiti.

This culture affected also music, movies, soundtracks, games.

With the popularity and legitimization of graffiti has come a level of commercialization. Some bigger companies even launched advertising campaigns and involved people to spray painting on sidewalks.

Graffiti have become a common stepping stone for many members of both the art and design communities. Artist made careers in skateboard, apparel, shoe design for companies.

It was actually a worldwide range process and global developments what we can meets also in South America, in Middle East or in Southeast Asia.

The modern-day graffiti artist can be found with an arsenal of various materials that allow for a successful production of a piece. From the different commodities comes different styles, technique and abilities to form master works of graffiti.
Some of the most common styles of graffiti have their own names.

Modern graffiti art often incorporates additional arts and technologies.
And in most of the places displayes graffiti as an decorative and high art.
